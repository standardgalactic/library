It provides mathematical formalisms and computational models to capture this navigation, offering insights into various cognitive phenomena such as place cell activity in the hippocampus, predictive coding, and therapeutic interventions.

The core of RAT lies in the concept of relevance activation: a process where the activation level of each cue influences the navigation trajectory based on its relevance to the current state. This relevance is dynamic and context-dependent, shaped by past experiences and current task demands.

Cognition is often conceptualized as an exploration or search in a high-dimensional state space. Authors: Jiangang Shan and Bradley R. Postle

Journal: Journal of Neuroscience (June 16, 2025)

DOI: 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.2414-24.2025

Summary:

This study by Jiangang Shan and Bradley Postle from the University of Wisconsin-Madison investigates the neural mechanisms underlying active removal of irrelevant information from visual working memory (WM). The research builds upon previous work that established two methods for removing WM content: passive removal, which involves withdrawing attention, and active removal, where information is functionally erased from WM.

Methodology:

The researchers conducted an experiment using the ABC-retrocuing task, in which participants memorized the orientation of colored bars on a screen and then had to discard one memory item (IMI) before another appeared. Participants performed this task while wearing electroencephalogram (EEG) sensors that recorded their brain activity. The researchers focused on two epochs: the triggering phase, when active removal was initiated, and the consequence phase, which evaluated the outcome of active removal.

Key Findings:

  1. Behavioral results showed that active removal led to a more pronounced decline in familiarity around the IMI compared to passive removal, indicating a more thorough erasure of information from WM.
  2. During the triggering phase, EEG recordings revealed stronger anterior-to-posterior traveling waves for active versus passive removal. These waves suggest a top-down signal originating in executive regions and propagating towards visual areas that initially encoded the now-irrelevant memory item.
  3. In the consequence phase, active removal resulted in reduced event-related potential (ERP) responses to task-irrelevant pings targeting sensory channels tuned to the discarded information. This finding implies decreased excitability or reduced gain in perceptual circuits associated with the IMI, consistent with successful downregulation.

Interpretation:

The authors propose a “hijacked adaptation” model for active removal from WM. According to this model, active removal involves top-down modulation of sensory circuit gain, effectively rendering them less sensitive to the no-longer-relevant information. This process mimics neural adaptation during sustained attention tasks and may represent a mechanism for preventing interference or intrusive thoughts.

Significance:

This study provides among the first direct neurophysiological evidence of active removal as a distinct cognitive operation, rather than simply passive neglect. These findings have potential implications for understanding mental health disorders involving intrusive thoughts and may inform therapeutic interventions targeting memory control processes in conditions such as depression, PTSD, and psychosis.

Additional Points:

  • The researchers compared active and passive removal to isolate the unique neural signatures of active deletion.
  • EEG data allowed them to observe real-time brain responses to the “forget” cue and probe for lingering neural traces using task-irrelevant pings.
  • The study’s findings suggest that the same neural mechanisms underlying attention control can also serve as a neural broom, actively pruning irrelevant WM content to maintain optimal performance.
