The researchers conducted an experiment using the ABC-retrocuing task, in which participants memorized the orientation of colored bars on a screen and then had to discard one memory item (IMI) before another appeared. The researchers focused on two epochs: the triggering phase, when active removal was initiated, and the consequence phase, which evaluated the outcome of active removal.

1. Behavioral results showed that active removal led to a more pronounced decline in familiarity around the IMI compared to passive removal, indicating a more thorough erasure of information from WM.

3. In the consequence phase, active removal resulted in reduced event-related potential (ERP) responses to task-irrelevant pings targeting sensory channels tuned to the discarded information. This finding implies decreased excitability or reduced gain in perceptual circuits associated with the IMI, consistent with successful downregulation.

This process mimics neural adaptation during sustained attention tasks and may represent a mechanism for preventing interference or intrusive thoughts.

**Significance:**

This study provides among the first direct neurophysiological evidence of active removal as a distinct cognitive operation, rather than simply passive neglect.
